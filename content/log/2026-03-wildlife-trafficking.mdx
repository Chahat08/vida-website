Wildlife trafficking is one of the largest illegal trades in the world, contributing to biodiversity loss, fueling organized crime, and raising public health concerns when animals are transported across borders with little oversight. For decades much of this trade occurred in physical markets. Today? A growing portion has moved online; animals and animal products are now advertised on major e-commerce platforms alongside everyday items like clothing or electronics. This shift in where animal trade happens has created some new challenges for law enforcement and conservation groups. But there’s one “pro” to all of this: it leaves behind a digital footprint that researchers are beginning to explore.

Every online listing contains bits of information — a product page might include a title, description, price, images, seller details, and a timestamp. While individually these pieces of data may not say much, at large scales they can reveal patterns about how wildlife products are bought and sold across the globe. But the difficulty of going through this data is the sheer volume of it, as online marketplaces host millions of listings and searching for the name of an animal often returns thousands of unrelated items. A search for “shark,” for example, might produce toys, artwork, clothing, or sports team merchandise. Sorting illegal wildlife advertisements from harmless products is time consuming for humans, and it is surprisingly complicated for automated systems. But a research team has developed a system designed to tackle that problem. By building a scalable pipeline that can collect and analyze wildlife-related advertisements across many platforms, regions, and languages, the team hopes to better understand how online wildlife trafficking works and how it might be disrupted.

The first step is web crawling. The researchers generate tens of thousands of search links by combining endangered species names with the search structures used by major e-commerce platforms, then a specialized “crawler” visits these pages and downloads the listings. The system limits how quickly it requests pages so it does not overwhelm the host websites, but the pipeline can still gather enormous datasets and in just over a month has collected more than 11 million online advertisements. Once the data are gathered, the next challenge is making sense of them as product pages are not standardized. Each website structures its listings differently, and sellers often describe products in inconsistent ways. But the pipeline uses automated tools to parse the raw web pages and extract useful details (such as titles, descriptions, prices, images, and seller information). These are then stored in structured formats that allow researchers to analyze the data at scale.
